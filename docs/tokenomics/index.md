# Contribution Economy Research

:::warning Research and test use only
SENEX does not operate a public token economy, token sale, staking product, or provider-payment network. **ASHA is a simulated, test-only accounting unit** used to study coordination scenarios. It is not transferable, tradeable, redeemable, or represented as having monetary value. Provider settlement and contribution rewards are not live.
:::

SENEX is researching how independently operated AIA systems could recognize useful contribution without turning private context into a commodity. The objective is a fair and auditable coordination model, not a promise of financial return.

## What the research is trying to solve

A contribution system must answer several questions without exposing sensitive implementation or user data:

- **What was authorized?** Participation must begin with an understandable, revocable grant.
- **What was useful?** Recognition should reflect a verified outcome rather than raw data volume or activity alone.
- **Who carried responsibility?** Operators and providers should be accountable for service quality, policy compliance, and failures.
- **Can the result be challenged?** Disputes need evidence, review, and a documented resolution path.
- **Can the system resist manipulation?** Repetition, identity abuse, collusion, fabricated work, and low-quality submissions must not become profitable strategies.
- **Can participation remain optional?** AIA should remain useful locally even when a person declines network contribution.

## Conceptual contribution lifecycle

The public model describes outcomes rather than proprietary scoring or verification methods:

1. **Consent** — a participant approves a bounded purpose, scope, and duration.
2. **Contribution** — an eligible service or knowledge contribution is performed under that authorization.
3. **Verification** — the system checks that the claimed outcome satisfies the applicable policy.
4. **Attribution** — qualifying participants receive an auditable record of recognition in the test environment.
5. **Review** — anomalous or disputed results can be held, examined, corrected, or rejected.
6. **Revocation and retention** — future use stops when authorization is withdrawn, subject to clearly stated integrity and legal obligations.

This lifecycle does not imply that raw personal data, private prompts, or proprietary model internals are published.

## Design principles

### Local value comes first

Participation in a shared network must not be a prerequisite for core local utility. Users should be able to understand when a task remains on their device and when an authorized interaction crosses a trust boundary.

### Recognition follows evidence

The design should recognize contributions only after the relevant outcome can be verified. It should not reward mere volume, speculation, or unverifiable claims.

### Rules are legible

Eligibility, review, and appeal rules should be understandable before participation. Material changes should be versioned and communicated.

### Safety outranks throughput

Questionable activity should be reviewable before it affects shared outcomes. A system that cannot safely explain or contest a decision is not ready for economic use.

### No implied financial rights

Simulation records do not confer ownership, equity, yield, redemption, future allocation, or entitlement to a production network.

## Public documentation boundary

These pages describe the intended accountability model. They intentionally do not publish scoring formulas, internal weights, abuse thresholds, settlement logic, security parameters, infrastructure topology, or operator-sensitive controls. Those details require controlled review and testing.
